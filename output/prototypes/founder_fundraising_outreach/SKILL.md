# Founder Fundraising Outreach

A skill that helps startup founders craft compelling investor outreach — cold emails, warm intros, follow-ups, due diligence prep docs, and pitch narratives that actually get replies.

## When to use

- "Help me write a cold email to a VC partner"
- "Draft a follow-up email after my investor meeting"
- "Prepare my due diligence documents for fundraising"
- "Write an intro blurb for a warm investor introduction"
- "Help me craft outreach for my seed round"

## How to use

### 1. Gather context

Before drafting, collect key information:

- **Company**: What does the startup do? What stage? What traction/metrics?
- **Round details**: Stage (pre-seed, seed, Series A), amount raising, valuation if set
- **Target investor**: Name, firm, thesis/focus areas, portfolio companies, any shared connections
- **Outreach type**: Cold email, warm intro request, follow-up, update, due diligence prep
- **Tone**: Founder's natural voice — direct, technical, narrative-driven, etc.

### 2. Draft the outreach

**Cold emails** should follow this structure:
- Subject line: specific, non-generic (reference their thesis, portfolio, or a metric)
- Opening line: why THIS investor (not a mass blast)
- One-liner: what you do, for whom, and why now
- Traction proof: 1-3 concrete metrics or milestones
- Ask: specific and low-friction (15-min call, not "invest in us")
- Signature: clean, with LinkedIn/website
- Total length: 5-8 sentences max

**Follow-ups** should:
- Reference the prior interaction specifically
- Add one new piece of traction or news
- Restate the ask clearly
- Be shorter than the original email

**Warm intro requests** should:
- Make it easy for the connector — include a forwardable blurb
- The blurb should be written in third person
- Include why the target investor is a fit

**Due diligence prep** should cover:
- Company overview and team bios
- Market size and competitive landscape
- Financial model summary and key assumptions
- Cap table overview
- Key risks and mitigations

### 3. Review and refine

- Remove jargon and buzzwords ("revolutionary", "disruptive", "AI-powered" without substance)
- Cut any sentence that doesn't earn its place
- Verify the tone matches the founder's voice
- Ensure every claim has a supporting metric or proof point
- Check that the ask is specific and actionable

### 4. Key principles

- **Brevity wins**: Investors skim. Every word must earn its place.
- **Specificity over flattery**: "I saw your investment in [Company X] and our approach to [Y] is adjacent" beats "I admire your portfolio."
- **Metrics over adjectives**: "40% MoM growth, 200 paying customers" beats "fast-growing with strong traction."
- **One CTA**: Don't ask for investment, feedback, AND intros in the same email.
- **No attachments in cold emails**: Link to a deck if needed, don't attach.
